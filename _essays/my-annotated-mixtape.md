### Introduction 

Paul Thomas Anderson’s signature filmmaking style is characterized by aspects such as thematic reoccurrence, camera technique, his undeniably engaging sense of humor, and, most importantly for the present essay, his musical choices. There is a throughline of sonic intentionality and significance to the unfolding of his narratives, both sprawling and intimate. As part of his auteurist approach to filmmaking, he has a long history of consistent musical collaborators, most notably Jonny Greenwood of Radiohead fame. Prior to the genesis of this particular partnership, he also worked with Jon Brion and Michael Penn a few times on his three nineties projects and 2002 rom-com *Punch-Drunk Love*. The scores produced for his films are every bit as committed to masterful storytelling as the scripts, actors, and production design, and while an in-depth exploration of Greenwood, Brion, and Penn’s contributions to the world of PTA would surely deliver engaging analysis, that is not my focus here. Instead, I was drawn to the less consistently utilized but equally engaging soundtracks of licensed, pre-existing songs in a few of his films. This interest was sparked after first watching Boogie Nights and recognizing that the mesmerizing effect the story had on me was due in part to the near-consuming count of (incredible) songs. Then came Licorice Pizza and Inherent Vice in my PTA education, whose soundtracks solidified a pattern of intense engagement with music history in his filmography. 

This feature project approaches the songs of these films—*Boogie Nights*,*Licorice Pizza*, and *Inherent Vice*—both with great distance and proximity. The diversification of perspective that the music-first analysis brings hopefully sheds new light on PTA’s films, aided by the multimedia inclusions of song embeds and data visualizations. The data visualizations serve primarily as a tool for seeing how these films’ soundtracks differ in their genre usage, movement in and out of the diegesis, and so on. The analysis below then functions as a looking glass into the specifics of each film, both building off the patterns displayed in the visualizations and moving beyond. From the glitz of disco to the blood of political violence, the music of these films holds history, meaning, and mood in its grasp.

### Method and Process

My process for this project took two distinct phases: data collection and data analysis. The “data” consist of the songs that play across the three films, and is accompanied by myriad metadata categories, including artist, release year, genre, status in the diegesis, and various quantitative scores measuring more technical aspects. 

The first step in data collection was compiling my own comprehensive and chronological playlist of the music featured in each film. Because official soundtracks are often neither complete, nor in order, this step was required to obtain the accurate aggregate of music I wanted. I first did as much research as possible across crowd-sourced sites such as <a href="https://www.tunefind.com/movie/boogie-nights-1997">Tunefind</a> and <a href=“https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0118749/soundtrack/?ref_=tt_ov_at_dyk_snd">IMDB</a>. While these existing efforts were incredibly helpful in identifying tracks and generally finding my way through the collection process, there was too much discrepancy between sources to make any sure conclusions. Therefore, I decided to take the most laborious and manual route and go through the films myself. This process also allowed me to record each song’s diegetic status—to the best of my ears’ ability. “Diegetic” refers to music that plays within the world of the film—the diegesis. This could be music from a car radio, speakers in a restaurant, a live band, and so on. The opposite, “extradiegetic,” is then music only the audience can hear. The least common occurrence is transdiegetic sound, meaning the music travels from being diegetic to extradiegetic, or vice versa. At times, it was difficult to decipher where the music was coming from and if changes to a track’s volume pointed at movement in and out of the diegesis or was simply a sound design choice. I typically took a sudden and extreme change in volume as a sign of transdiegetic movement, which I only came across a few times.

Once the playlist was complete, I used <a href=“https://exportify.net/">Exportify</a>—a web application that accesses the Spotify Web API for users via their account login—to get a CSV (Comma-Separated Values) of the available metadata. The Web API (Application Programming Interface) is an interface that allows for data retrieval and sharing, often meant to be a sort of steering wheel in applications that deal with Spotify data, such as the music social platform <a href=“https://stats.fm/">stats.fm</a>. There were some things to consider, however, given my method of metadata collection. For one, the genre labels attached to each song were up to the discretion of Spotify—a categorization process whose logic I am not privy to. Given this project’s interest in cultural context, I wanted to emphasize this possible pitfall, as there might be much nuance lost in Spotify’s genre labeling. Beyond this larger consideration, I came across a couple of smaller issues. For one, during my playlist compiling, I had not been thinking about an automated metadata process and therefore added a few remastered songs in place of their originals. Because of this, some of the release year metadata reflected the remastered dates as opposed to the original releases; this was an easy fix, though, as I simply scanned the dates in the spreadsheet and replaced them.

With a completed playlist and metadata sheet, I began experimenting with different modes of visualizing the data. The kind of comparison I was most interested in was based on proportional differences—which patterns exist in the data, and what were the trends in genre, diegesis status, and so on. Because of this particular visualization goal, I went for the ever-classic donut chart to highlight percentages, as opposed to the specific song counts (plus, the donut charts look a bit like LPs). I used the data visualization templates from the tool <a href=“https://flourish.studio/">Flourish</a> to create my graphs, as this was an easy way to create embeddable and, most importantly, interactive visualizations. Given the number of graphs I wanted to incorporate in my project, alongside the fact that the main portion of the work was ultimately writing my analysis, I thought it best to utilize a no-code platform as opposed to tinkering around with my own coding. That way, I would have the space and time to easily create new visualizations as they felt relevant to what I was writing. At the heart of this process was a desired mobility; I wanted the steps to blur together a bit to really capture a conversation between the visualizations and the writing. For this reason, while the writing portion was theoretically “last,” the completion of the project was more akin to a see-saw movement than any linear progression. I conducted research, took notes, wrote blurbs, and returned to the visualizations in varied succession.

### A Closer Look at the Films

#### Mixtape Side A: *Boogie Nights*

Boogie Nights might be the first thing one thinks of when considering the significance of music to Anderson’s filmmaking. The entire film is steeped in music, both in its near ceaseless soundtracking and in the thematic importance of disco to the world of the characters. The title even comes from a 1976 disco track of the same name by the band Heatwave. This song was originally intended to feature in the film; however, the devoutly Christian lead singer, Johnnie Wilder, Jr., didn’t want his music to be used in a film about the porn industry[^1]. Beyond the genre of disco, though, the significance of music in Boogie Nights centers around the sociality of the film: the workplace culture intertwined with partying (boogie-ing!) and, ultimately, the formation of an alternative family unit.

##### *Track #1: Disco, or, "The Politics of, Ohhh, Feeling Good"[^2]*

Both visually and aurally, the opening of Boogie Nights establishes disco as fundamental to the film’s sound, aesthetic, and sub-cultural sentiments. The iconic tracking shot that introduces much of the ensemble cast in the Hot Traxx nightclub spans almost three whole minutes of disco-entrenched glitz, glamour, and gallivanting. By means of the three consecutive disco tracks overheard in the nightclub, the characters are not simply first shown but introduced within this specific cultural context; their narrative grounding and social involvement in this space, and therefore association with the sounds of disco, frame their lives. Hot Traxx being a social and business hub for the characters is more than simply setting the scene of the era. Instead, it speaks to an inherent thematic correlation between the world of disco and the world of the adult film industry. While certainly separate spheres of life with individual origins and governing ideologies, there exists ties between disco and the porn industry given their shared explorations of the body, sexuality, and counter (or not)-culture—despite, perhaps, divergent successes in these explorations, and their societal consequences.

Disco’s inherent sexuality is expressed through various modes. Like much of popular music spanning the past many decades, romance and sex are simply common topics for disco lyrics. The explicitness of the sexual references, however, greatly ranges. Overall, disco probably isn’t any more explicit than rock of the era, and certainly not of modern pop and rap. The essentiality of sex and the body to disco is founded instead on its purpose as dance music and its subcultural roots in queer and POC-lead dance spaces. In Richard Dyer’s 1979 article “In Defense of Disco” for Gay Left, a London-based socialist journal, the queer theorist and film studies professor offers an argument about disco as a vessel for a liberatory re-formulation of music’s sexuality [^3]. He posits that the rhythm (or lack thereof) of rock music is inherently phallo-centric, based in a strained aggression which is physically expressed in thrusts. In turn, the way disco asks you to dance is a more fluid connection to the body, regardless of gender or what body one might be occupying. The difference between disco and other forms of popular music boils down to an allowance and embracement of physicality, in Dyer’s argument. How one should exist in and use their body is a question that is, of course, massively relevant to the world of adult films and sex work. The historical ties between homosexuality and prostitution ground the societal disapproval of sex work in more than a question of exploitation or feminism, and remind us to examine taboo not as objective immorality but as the product of a culture’s prejudices.

However, the question of exploitation is nevertheless incredibly important—for the disco industry, not only pornography. An article from the *New York Times* published in 1979 discusses the complexities of agency in entertainment: “Disco music, like the ‘girl group’ sound perfected by such producers as Phil Spector, is ultimately a producer's music, which means men's music, which means the exploitation of women to suit male fantasies…”[^4] While this article certainly doesn’t formulate a perfect argument, ultimately falling a bit into the very misogyny it is trying to examine, it offers a perspective published by media of the time that acknowledges the layers present in any commercial form’s attempts to disrupt perceptions of female sexuality. Like with sex work, the conversation on agency versus the male gaze—what is truly possible under such comprehensive patriarchy—clearly existed in the disco industry since its heyday in the 70s. And like the theme of changing technologies and consumption in *Boogie Nights*, it is the intense commercialization and mass-production of something that exacerbates these issues.

#### Mixtape #6B: *Licorice Pizza*

Licorice Pizza, same as Boogie Nights, is star-studded with fantastic needledrops. It distinguishes itself alongside its partner in PTA’s filmography as using primarily non-original music, cementing the period aspect of the film. Besides its function of capturing the 70s, the soundtrack is deftly used to match—and define, at times—the mood of both the romance and the business in the plot.

#### Mixtape #6C: *Inherent Vice* 

While maybe not as immediately obvious as with Boogie Nights and its disco-club locale, Inherent Vice is similarly engaging with a period of music history from which its narrative is informed. I’d even argue that the music history being referenced in this trippy neo-noir is uncomplicated—more staunchly at the thematic forefront—in comparison, as Boogie Nights’ focus on the adult film industry ultimately obscures the theoretical “takeaways” one could make on the film’s alignment with disco. With Inherent Vice, the very heart of the film’s narrative is in direct and undisturbed conversation with music history; that is, a story about the 60s being over is a story contained within and chronicled by music.
